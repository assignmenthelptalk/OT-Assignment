Stroke Types and Consequences — What OT Assignments Must Address

Ischaemic stroke, comprising 85% of all strokes, results from arterial occlusion — either thrombotic (clot formed in situ) or embolic (clot travelling from elsewhere). Left hemisphere ischaemic stroke is more common and typically produces right hemiplegia alongside aphasia or dysphasia. Right hemisphere ischaemic stroke more commonly produces left hemiplegia with unilateral neglect. Haemorrhagic stroke, comprising 15%, results from arterial rupture — either intracerebral (within brain tissue) or subarachnoid (around the brain). Haemorrhagic strokes typically present as more severe and follow a different rehabilitation trajectory. Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA), where symptoms resolve within 24 hours, is relevant to OT in the context of secondary prevention — risk factor education, activity modification, and lifestyle adjustment.

Motor consequences include hemiplegia (complete paralysis) and hemiparesis (partial weakness) contralateral to the lesion side. These affect ADL performance, transfers, and mobility — the core content of OT assessment in stroke case studies. Perceptual consequences include unilateral neglect and hemianopia. Unilateral neglect — failure to attend to stimuli on the contralesional side — is more common after right parietal cortex stroke and produces left-sided ADL difficulties. Hemianopia is visual field loss. These two conditions must be distinguished in case study assignments because they require different OT interventions. Communication consequences include aphasia (impairment of language processing — understanding, reading, writing, and speaking) and dysphasia (impairment of speech production specifically). Both affect OT assessment validity: COPM requires verbal response; Barthel Index can be completed by observation; AMPS is performance-based and least affected by communication impairment.

Dysphagia (swallowing difficulty) is relevant to OT kitchen and meal preparation assessment. Cognitive consequences — memory impairment, attention deficits across sustained, selective, and divided attention subtypes, and executive function changes affecting planning, initiation, and organisation — all affect occupational performance and must be addressed in case study assignments through the MoCA cognitive screen and its implications for assessment validity. Psychological consequences including emotional lability, post-stroke depression, and post-stroke fatigue affect engagement in OT and must be acknowledged in the occupational profile section of the case study.

Unilateral Neglect and Hemianopia — Assessment and Intervention Implications for OT Assignments

Unilateral neglect is defined as failure to attend to stimuli on the contralesional side of space, despite intact sensory pathways — it is not a vision problem. This is a critical distinction that examiners test at Level 6: students who describe neglect as a visual impairment misidentify the clinical mechanism. Unilateral neglect is most common after right parietal cortex stroke and affects ADL performance (incomplete left-sided grooming, bumping into objects on the left), transfers, and mobility. Assessment tools referenced in OT case studies include the star cancellation test and behavioural inattention test. OT interventions address scanning training, environmental cueing, and graduated attentional retraining.

Hemianopia is visual field loss resulting from damage to the visual cortex or optic tract — it is a sensory impairment, not an attentional one. Both conditions may co-exist post-stroke but require different interventions, and the case study must name which is present and justify the distinct intervention approach for each.

Post-Stroke Cognitive Changes — Executive Function, Memory and Attention in OT Case Studies

Executive function changes affect initiation of ADLs, task sequencing, and safety during home-based activities. A client who understands dressing instructions but cannot sequence the steps independently demonstrates executive function impairment at the planning and initiation level. The MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment) screens for cognitive impairment with a cut-off score of 25 or below out of 30. When a stroke case study client presents with a MoCA of 25 or below, the assignment must address two specific clinical reasoning points: whether the impaired cognition affects assessment validity — can this client self-report reliably for a COPM interview? — and whether the intervention approach can be learned — can this client acquire new compensatory techniques given memory impairment?

Attention subtypes produce different functional consequences. Sustained attention impairment reduces the client's ability to maintain focus through a multi-step ADL task. Selective attention impairment reduces the ability to screen out distractions during task performance. Divided attention impairment affects multi-tasking, which is relevant to complex IADL performance — cooking requires simultaneous monitoring of multiple ongoing tasks. Each attention subtype requires different OT intervention strategies and must be named separately in the case study when relevant.

OT Stroke Assessments — Scoring, Interpretation and Documentation

The Barthel Index measures ten ADL items — feeding, bathing, grooming, upper and lower body dressing, bowel control, bladder control, toilet use, transfers between bed and chair, mobility on a level surface, and stair negotiation. Items are scored 0, 5, 10, or 15 depending on the item, giving a total score range of 0–100. Interpretation bands provide the clinical framework for case study goal-setting and intervention planning: a score of 0–20 indicates total dependence; 21–60 indicates severe dependence; 61–90 indicates moderate dependence; 91–99 indicates slight dependence; 100 indicates full independence. Students most commonly lose marks by reporting a Barthel score without interpreting what it means functionally for the client and without connecting it to specific OT goals and interventions. A Barthel of 45 and a Barthel of 85 require fundamentally different OT goal structures and intervention priorities.

The FIM (Functional Independence Measure) covers 18 items — 13 motor and 5 cognitive — scored on a 7-point scale per item: 7 = complete independence, 6 = modified independence, 5 = supervision or setup, 4 = minimal assist, 3 = moderate assist, 2 = maximal assist, 1 = total assist. The motor subtotal ranges from 13 to 91; the cognitive subtotal ranges from 5 to 35; the total FIM score ranges from 18 to 126. FIM is used primarily in inpatient rehabilitation for admission-discharge outcome comparison, capturing small incremental functional changes that the Barthel's larger score intervals may miss.

The AMPS (Assessment of Motor and Process Skills) assesses the quality of occupational performance rather than task completion. It covers 16 motor skill items and 20 process skill items. Thresholds for independent functional performance are: ADL motor score of 2.0 logits or above, and ADL process score of 1.0 logits or above — these exact values must appear in any case study that references AMPS. AMPS requires a certified rater: a student on placement is not qualified to administer AMPS independently. Assignments incorporating AMPS data must reflect this administrative constraint when describing who conducted the assessment.

The Rivermead ADL Assessment covers 15 items across self-care and household tasks on an ordinal scale, used in subacute and community stroke rehabilitation settings. The COPM applies client-centred priority identification with performance and satisfaction scales of 1–10; clinically significant change requires at least a 2-point improvement. The MoCA (0–30; cut-off 25) screens for cognitive impairment prior to formal OT assessment. The DASH (Disabilities of the Arm, Shoulder and Hand; 0–100; 0 = no disability, 100 = most severe) measures upper limb function specifically when upper limb rehabilitation is the primary OT focus.

Barthel Index Interpretation — Applying Score Bands to Stroke OT Case Studies

A Barthel score of 0–20 indicates total dependence: OT priorities at this level are carer education, positioning to prevent secondary complications, equipment prescription for supported function, and early goal-setting with the client and family. A Barthel score of 21–60 indicates severe dependence: OT priorities shift to transfer training, basic ADL training for feeding and grooming, and home assessment planning for eventual discharge. A Barthel score of 61–90 indicates moderate dependence: OT priorities address specific ADL skill training — one-handed dressing, shower seat use, adapted equipment — alongside fatigue management and home modification recommendations. A Barthel score of 91–99 indicates slight dependence: OT priorities focus on upper limb rehabilitation to address the residual functional limitation, and return to complex IADLs such as cooking and community mobility. A Barthel score of 100 indicates independence in the ten assessed ADL items: OT priorities in this case shift to chronic phase concerns — driving rehabilitation, return to work, and sustained community participation.

AMPS and FIM — When to Use Which Tool in a Stroke OT Assignment

FIM is appropriate in inpatient rehabilitation settings when admission-to-discharge outcome comparison is the required format — its 7-point scale captures incremental functional changes in patients with severe presentations where the Barthel's 5-point intervals are insufficient to demonstrate progress. AMPS is appropriate when the quality of occupational performance is the assessment question — not merely whether the client can complete the task, but how efficiently and safely they perform it, including process skill organisation and motor skill execution. In assignments, the selection of FIM versus AMPS must be explicitly justified by the clinical setting and assessment purpose. AMPS's requirement for a certified rater is a setting and assignment constraint that students must acknowledge — stating "the AMPS was administered by the certified OT supervisor" or equivalent.

OT Stroke Interventions — Evidence-Based Approaches and Protocols

OT stroke interventions employ task-specific and occupation-based approaches with evidence levels ranging from Level 1A systematic review evidence (CIMT, ADL retraining) to moderate evidence (bilateral training, mirror therapy). Each intervention description in a stroke case study assignment must include: what the intervention is, its evidence level, its protocol parameters where established, the phase of stroke rehabilitation in which it is indicated, and the specific occupational performance outcomes it targets.

CIMT (Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy) constrains the unaffected upper limb using a mitt or sling worn 90% of waking hours — approximately 13–14 hours per day — while the affected limb is trained through 3–6 hours of massed practice on task-specific activities per day, over a 10–15 day protocol. CIMT carries Level 1A evidence from multiple Cochrane reviews. Its mechanism involves forced use of the affected limb combined with massed practice, which drives cortical reorganisation in the affected hemisphere.

Task-specific training involves repetitive practice of meaningful functional tasks with progressive difficulty grading. It is directly linked to the top-down, occupation-first approach and carries Level 1A evidence for ADL outcomes post-stroke. Bilateral training involves simultaneous symmetrical movement of both upper limbs and is used when CIMT criteria are not met — it carries moderate evidence.

ADL retraining follows either a top-down approach (practicing the whole meaningful task in context, using compensatory techniques immediately rather than waiting for underlying deficit remediation) or a bottom-up approach (remediating the underlying motor or cognitive deficit first, then progressing to functional task practice). The top-down approach has stronger evidence for functional ADL outcomes. Mirror therapy places a mirror at the body midline so that the reflection of the unaffected limb creates the visual illusion of the affected limb moving — sessions of 15–30 minutes are appropriate for hemiplegia when active movement is not yet possible, with evidence supporting upper limb motor recovery in the acute and subacute phases.

Fatigue management employs energy conservation techniques — activity prioritisation, rest breaks, and task modification — alongside pacing (activity monitoring within the client's energy envelope) and the PERT (Post-stroke Fatigue Management Programme), a structured 6-session group or individual format with documented efficacy. Driving rehabilitation is a specialised OT domain applicable in the chronic phase: it requires on-road assessment and carries the legal requirement of DVLA notification following stroke in the UK. Home modification — environmental assessment producing handrail, grab rail, level access, and bedroom or bathroom adaptation recommendations — is relevant in the subacute phase and at discharge.

CIMT Protocol — What OT Students Must Know for Stroke Assignments

CIMT protocol parameters carry specific eligibility criteria that must be stated in any stroke assignment that recommends CIMT. The minimum eligibility threshold is 10 degrees of active wrist extension and some active finger extension in the affected limb. CIMT is therefore not appropriate for total hemiplegia — this is the most commonly missed clinical reasoning point in student CIMT descriptions. Stating that CIMT is a recommended intervention without specifying eligibility criteria and confirming the client meets them represents clinical reasoning absent of the assessment evidence that justifies the intervention choice. This omission consistently costs marks at Level 6. CIMT is also contraindicated in the hyperacute and early acute phases (first two weeks post-stroke) due to medical instability, and in patients with significant cognitive impairment that prevents engagement in massed practice.

Top-Down vs Bottom-Up Approaches in Stroke ADL Retraining

The top-down approach starts with the whole meaningful occupation — dressing, cooking, transferring — and practices the complete task in context from the outset. Compensatory strategies are introduced immediately rather than waiting for bilateral hand function to recover. An example: the client uses one-handed dressing technique immediately post-stroke rather than completing hand-strengthening exercises before attempting dressing. This approach aligns with occupation-based OT philosophy and has the stronger evidence base for functional ADL outcomes at the subacute and chronic phases.

The bottom-up approach remediates the underlying impairment first — motor retraining, sensory re-education, cognitive rehabilitation — before progressing to functional task practice. An example: hand-strengthening and fine motor training before returning the client to kitchen activities. This approach is appropriate when specific remediable deficits are identified through assessment and the stroke phase makes active neurological recovery probable.

OT case study assignments at Level 6 and Level 7 must explicitly justify the choice of top-down versus bottom-up using assessment data — Barthel score, AMPS results, stroke phase — and evidence. The justification must acknowledge the alternative approach and explain why the evidence and assessment data support the chosen direction. "The top-down approach was selected because..." requires a completion that references the assessment findings, not merely the philosophical preference.

Stroke Phases and OT Role — Hyperacute to Chronic

The hyperacute phase, defined as the first 24 hours post-stroke, limits OT assessment due to medical instability. OT role in this phase centres on positioning to prevent contracture and pressure ulcers, early support with swallowing screen for dysphagia, sensory stimulation, and family communication. Full standardised assessment is not appropriate in the hyperacute phase — assignments must reflect this clinical reality.

The acute phase, defined as 24 hours to 2 weeks post-stroke, establishes the OT assessment baseline. OT role includes bedside ADL assessment using the Barthel Index, early mobilisation, cognitive screening with the MoCA, early goal-setting with the client and family, carer education, and preventing secondary complications including shoulder pain and contracture formation.

The subacute phase, defined as 2 weeks to 6 months post-stroke, constitutes the period of most intensive OT rehabilitation. OT role includes full assessment using AMPS, COPM, and DASH where relevant; CIMT if eligibility criteria are met; intensive ADL retraining; cognitive rehabilitation if indicated by MoCA and functional assessment findings; fatigue management; pre-discharge home visit assessment; and home modification recommendations. The majority of the evidence base for OT stroke interventions addresses the subacute phase.

The chronic phase, defined as greater than 6 months post-stroke, shifts OT focus toward community integration and maintenance. OT role in the chronic phase includes community integration programmes, driving rehabilitation assessment and DVLA notification, return to work assessment, complex IADL retraining, assistive technology prescription, and long-term adjustment support. A subacute phase case study that sets driving rehabilitation as a goal demonstrates a phase-mismatch clinical reasoning error that examiners at Level 6 will identify.

Structuring a Stroke OT Case Study Assignment — From Occupational Profile to Intervention Evaluation

A stroke OT case study assignment follows a five-section structure that maps the clinical content covered on this page to each academic component. Section 1 — Occupational Profile — establishes the stroke history, social and occupational context, and initial functional presentation. This is where stroke type, hemisphere of lesion, and presenting consequences are named. Section 2 — Assessment — documents the standardised assessment scores with interpretation: Barthel score and band, FIM motor and cognitive subtotals, COPM performance and satisfaction scores, MoCA result and its implication for assessment validity.

Section 3 — Clinical Reasoning — identifies the occupational performance problems from assessment data, prioritises them by risk and functional impact, and explains why specific assessment findings lead to specific problem identification. Section 4 — Goal-Setting and Intervention Plan — presents SMART goals linked directly to assessment findings, with evidence-based intervention selection including the evidence level, protocol parameters (CIMT waking hours, daily practice duration), and phase justification. Section 5 — Evaluation — describes the planned outcome measures and the clinically meaningful change thresholds (COPM change of at least 2 points; Barthel functional band transition). Clinical Reasoning in Stroke OT Case Studies — Connecting Assessment to Intervention

Clinical reasoning in stroke OT case studies requires three reasoning types applied explicitly to the clinical content. Procedural reasoning analyses observed ADL performance difficulties — using assessment scores to identify what the client cannot do and why. A Barthel of 45 indicates severe dependence and directs procedural reasoning toward transfer training and basic ADL retraining priorities. Conditional reasoning projects forward — given the assessment data and the stroke phase, what functional trajectory is probable? A subacute client with active upper limb movement who meets CIMT eligibility criteria carries a different functional prognosis than a hyperacute client with complete hemiplegia. Narrative reasoning understands what rehabilitation means to this specific client — what does returning to cooking or driving represent for their occupational identity? This reasoning type connects clinical goal-setting to the client's occupational narrative and is assessed as clinical reasoning quality at Level 6.

Students demonstrating distinction-level clinical reasoning connect assessment data to intervention selection with explicit explanations at each step: "The Barthel score of 45 indicates severe dependence, directing initial OT goals toward transfer and basic ADL retraining (procedural reasoning). The client's active wrist extension of 15 degrees meets CIMT eligibility criteria; conditional reasoning, drawing on Cochrane review evidence of neurological plasticity in the subacute phase, supports CIMT as an adjunct to ADL retraining. The client's stated goal of returning to cooking for the family reflects narrative reasoning — the productivity domain occupies primary occupational meaning." FAQ — Stroke Occupational Therapy Assignment Questions

What Barthel Index score should I use in a stroke OT case study to demonstrate meaningful rehabilitation progress?

Clinically meaningful change on the Barthel Index is generally considered to be at least 4–5 points in research literature, but this depends on the admission score and the client's trajectory. A patient who starts at 20 (total dependence) and improves to 45 (severe dependence) has made substantial progress — a transition across two dependence bands with independence achieved in feeding and grooming. In assignments, state the admission and discharge Barthel scores, calculate the change score, and interpret it functionally: "a change of 25 points representing transition from total to severe dependence, with independence now achieved in feeding, grooming, and upper body dressing." Interpretation in relation to the client's occupational goals — not just the score change — is what earns marks at Level 6.

When is CIMT not appropriate for a stroke patient in an OT case study?

CIMT is contraindicated when the patient has total hemiplegia — the minimum eligibility criterion is 10 degrees of active wrist extension and some active finger movement. It is also not appropriate in the hyperacute or early acute phase (first two weeks) due to medical instability; in patients with significant cognitive impairment preventing engagement in massed practice; or when the patient declines the treatment. Students must justify intervention eligibility explicitly in case study assignments — stating CIMT is recommended without confirming the client meets the wrist extension criterion is a clinical reasoning omission that loses marks on assessment-to-intervention connection criteria.

How do I write about unilateral neglect in a stroke OT assignment?

Define unilateral neglect accurately: failure to attend to stimuli on the contralesional side, not a visual problem. It is more common after right parietal cortex damage and manifests as left-sided ADL difficulties — incomplete left-sided grooming, bumping into objects on the left, reading from the right half of a page only. Describe assessment approaches used in OT practice — star cancellation, behavioural inattention test — and OT interventions: scanning training, environmental cueing, and mirror feedback. Explicitly distinguish neglect from hemianopia — an examiner at Level 6 expects this distinction as a clinical reasoning quality marker.

What is the difference between aphasia and dysphasia in an OT stroke case study?

Aphasia is impairment of language processing — it affects understanding, reading, writing, and speaking; caused by left hemisphere stroke in most right-handed individuals. Dysphasia is impairment specifically of speech production — the client may understand language but cannot produce words. The critical clinical reasoning point for OT case study assignments is what this means for assessment validity: COPM requires verbal response and may require adapted administration; Barthel Index can be completed by direct observation; AMPS is performance-based and is the least affected by communication impairment. The student must address how communication impairment affects assessment choice and document this in the assessment rationale section of the case study.

Should my stroke OT case study use a top-down or bottom-up approach — which gets better marks?

Neither is inherently better — the mark is gained by justifying the choice using assessment data and evidence. A top-down approach is better justified when the stroke is in the subacute or chronic phase, when assessment shows moderate functional independence (Barthel 61–90), and when the client's occupational goals are specific and achievable. A bottom-up approach is better justified when specific remediable impairments are identified in the early subacute phase and active neurological recovery is expected. The best distinction assignments justify the approach chosen, acknowledge the alternative, and explain why the evidence and assessment data support the chosen direction — demonstrating conditional reasoning rather than stylistic preference.
