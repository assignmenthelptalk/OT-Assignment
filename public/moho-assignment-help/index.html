What Is MOHO? The Model of Human Occupation Explained for OT Students

MOHO — the Model of Human Occupation — was developed by Gary Kielhofner and originally published in 1980, with substantial revisions through the 4th edition (Kielhofner, 2008) and continuing theoretical development. MOHO frames human beings as occupational beings whose health and well-being are fundamentally connected to their engagement in occupation. It is an open-systems model: the person is an open system whose occupational behaviour emerges from the dynamic interaction between three internal constructs (volition, habituation, and performance capacity) and the environment in which occupation takes place.

MOHO differs from biomechanical and neurological frames of reference in a fundamental way: it addresses not only what a person can physically do (performance capacity) but also why they choose to engage or disengage from occupations (volition), how their daily occupational patterns are organised (habituation), and how their environment enables or constrains occupational participation. This makes MOHO the appropriate model for any assignment where the clinical question goes beyond physical function to encompass occupational meaning, role disruption, motivational deficits, or the impact of contextual factors on occupational engagement. In assignments, examiners expect MOHO to be applied — every construct connected to specific case evidence — not merely mentioned. Writing "MOHO was used to inform the assessment process" without connecting volition, habituation, performance capacity, and environment to specific case data will not achieve distinction-level marks.

MOHO Core Constructs: Volition, Habituation, and Performance Capacity

Volition is the motivational construct within MOHO — it explains why a person chooses to engage in, avoid, or approach occupations. Volition comprises three sub-constructs that must each be defined with specificity in assignments.

Volition — Personal Causation, Values, and Interests

Personal causation refers to the individual's sense of their own capacity and effectiveness in occupations. It comprises two components: the sense of capacity (the person's belief in their own ability to perform specific tasks — "I can do this") and the sense of efficacy (the person's belief that they can achieve desired outcomes through their actions — "my effort will make a difference"). In case study assignments, personal causation is evidenced by COPM performance scores — a client who rates their performance at 3/10 for a task they previously performed without difficulty is demonstrating diminished personal causation even when objective physical capacity may partially support the task. A client who refuses to attempt cooking post-stroke despite adequate grip strength is expressing a personal causation deficit that biomechanical assessment will not capture.

Values in MOHO comprise convictions (what the person believes is important and worth doing) and commitments (how the person feels obligated to act in relation to their occupational roles and responsibilities). Values determine which occupations carry meaning and priority for the client — they are what COPM importance ratings (1–10) measure. A client who rates cooking 9/10 for importance is expressing a strong values-based connection to that occupation, making it the appropriate priority for intervention planning.

Interests comprise attraction (what draws a person toward specific occupations) and enjoyment (the subjective experience of pleasure or satisfaction during occupational engagement). Interests are documented in the occupational history section of the occupational profile — what the client found enjoyable before condition onset, and whether those interests remain accessible or have been disrupted.

Habituation — Roles and Habits

Habituation is the structural construct within MOHO — it explains how occupational behaviour is organised and patterned into daily life through internalised roles and habits.

Roles are internalised sets of behaviours associated with a social position (parent, worker, student, carer, spouse). Roles operate as scripts — they define which occupations a person is expected to perform, in which contexts, and with what regularity. When a significant role is disrupted by illness, injury, or life change, the occupations associated with that role are also disrupted. In assignment writing, the worker role loss following stroke explains not only the absence of work tasks but the disruption to all the occupations associated with the work context — commuting, professional relationships, the daily structure provided by employment.

Habits are acquired tendencies to respond in familiar ways in familiar contexts — they operate largely without conscious attention. The morning self-care routine, the sequence of medication management, the pattern of meal preparation — these are habitual occupational behaviours that require minimal cognitive resources when intact, and create significant disruption when the sequence is interrupted by new physical or cognitive limitations. The distinction between roles (WHAT occupations) and habits (HOW those occupations are performed) is a common assignment error — confusing them reduces marks on theoretical model application criteria.

Performance Capacity — Objective and Subjective Dimensions

Performance capacity in MOHO comprises two distinct components. The objective component encompasses the underlying musculoskeletal, neurological, cardiopulmonary, and cognitive capacities that enable occupation — measured by standardised assessment tools (AMPS motor and process logit scores, FIM item scores, grip dynamometry, cognitive assessments such as MoCA). The subjective component — the lived body experience — captures how it feels to perform an occupation: the phenomenological experience of doing.

The subjective lived body is MOHO's unique theoretical contribution to understanding performance capacity. A client post-stroke may have measurable grip strength (objective: 12kg dynamometry) and adequate ROM, but may describe their affected arm as "feeling like it belongs to someone else" — an alienated, unreliable, no longer-familiar body part. Standard assessments do not capture this subjective experience, but MOHO requires the occupational therapist to address it in the occupational formulation. In assignments, the subjective lived body is documented in the occupational profile narrative using the client's own language about how their body feels during attempted occupation.

The MOHO Environment: Physical, Social, and Occupational

Environment in MOHO is not a background context for occupation — it is an active shaper of occupational engagement that interacts dynamically with volition, habituation, and performance capacity. MOHO's environment construct comprises three dimensions that must each be addressed in assignments with specificity, not vague generalisation.

The physical environment encompasses natural environments, built spaces, and the objects used in occupation. In assignments, the physical environment is documented with specific detail: "the client lives in a second-floor flat with 14 steps and no lift — this removes the occupational form of 'going to the local shops independently,' previously performed daily and integral to the client's social engagement role." Stating "the client lives in a flat" is not MOHO-level environment analysis.

The social environment encompasses the social groups the client participates in (family, colleagues, community) and the occupational forms — socially defined, culturally shared sequences of doing — available to the client. Occupational forms are a MOHO-specific concept: they are the shared cultural scripts that define how activities are typically performed in a given social context (a "family meal" is an occupational form; "eating alone at a desk" is a different occupational form). The social environment determines which occupational forms are available, expected, and accessible.

The occupational environment — sometimes framed as the opportunities, demands, and constraints within the environment — addresses what occupations the client can engage in, is expected to engage in, and is restricted from engaging in within their current context. Environmental press (the demands the environment makes on the person's performance capacity) is relevant here: a high-rise flat without a lift creates a different occupational environment from a ground-floor property with level access.

MOHO-Based Assessments: MOHOST, OPHI-II, OCAIRS, WRI, and OSA

MOHO generates a battery of five primary assessment tools, each designed to measure different MOHO constructs in different clinical contexts. Students who write "the MOHOST was administered" without stating the ratings and their clinical implications are not demonstrating EAV depth — they are describing an assessment without documenting it. Each tool's scoring system must appear in the assignment with specific values.

MOHOST — The Model of Human Occupation Screening Tool

MOHOST measures occupational functioning across six sections that directly map to MOHO's four constructs. Each section is rated using a four-point qualitative scale:

MOHOST Rating Full Name Meaning Assignment Implication
F Facilitates The factor actively supports and enables occupational engagement Strength to build intervention on; document as a resource
A Allows The factor is neutral — does not create significant barriers Maintenance goal; not a priority intervention target
I Inhibits The factor limits or disrupts occupational engagement Priority intervention target — link to MOHO construct and intervention approach
R Restricts The factor severely compromises occupational engagement Highest priority intervention target — immediate clinical focus

The six MOHOST sections and their MOHO construct mappings are: (1) Motivation for Occupation → volition construct; (2) Pattern of Occupation → habituation construct; (3) Communication and Interaction Skills → performance capacity (communication); (4) Process Skills → performance capacity (process); (5) Motor Skills → performance capacity (motor); (6) Environment → environment construct. A client with depression who remains in bed until midday would receive an Inhibits (I) rating on Pattern of Occupation, reflecting disrupted habits and absent productive role occupations. This rating connects directly to the habituation construct in the theoretical model application section of the case study.

MOHOST administration uses structured observation and interview and can be completed from observation alone in inpatient settings where interview is not feasible. It is widely used across mental health and physical rehabilitation settings and is the most common MOHO-based assessment in UK OT practice.

OPHI-II — Occupational Performance History Interview

OPHI-II (Occupational Performance History Interview, 2nd edition) comprises three rating scales and a narrative component:

  • Occupational Identity Scale — 11 items, score range 11–44 (4-point scale per item: 4 = exceptionally positive, 3 = appropriate, 2 = some difficulty, 1 = extreme difficulty)
  • Occupational Competence Scale — 9 items, score range 9–36 (same 4-point scale)
  • Occupational Behaviour Settings Scale — 9 items, score range 9–36 (same 4-point scale)
  • Life History Narrative Form — captures key life events, changes to daily routines, and their impact on occupational identity over time; provides the qualitative narrative that MOHO's occupational identity construct requires

The semi-structured interview covers three domains: occupational roles (past and present), daily routine (organisation of daily occupational patterns), and occupational behaviour settings (physical and social environments of occupation). OPHI-II is best suited to assignments where the occupational history over time is clinically important — chronic mental health conditions, progressive neurological conditions, major life transitions. When the assignment requires demonstrating change in occupational identity over time, OPHI-II provides the assessment structure. In assignments, OPHI-II scale scores must be stated per section alongside interpretation — not just "the OPHI-II was administered."

OCAIRS, WRI, and OSA — Additional MOHO Assessment Tools

OCAIRS (Occupational Circumstances Assessment Interview and Rating Scale) rates 12 domains on a 4-point scale (1–4): Personal Causation, Values, Interests, Roles, Habits, Skills, Short-term Goals, Long-term Goals, Interpretation of Past Experience, Physical Environment, Social Environment, and Readiness for Change. This direct mapping of MOHO constructs to domain ratings makes OCAIRS particularly clear for assignment documentation — each rating connects explicitly to a MOHO construct. OCAIRS is interview-based and widely used in community mental health and forensic OT settings.

WRI (Worker Role Interview) is specifically designed for vocational rehabilitation contexts — assessing MOHO constructs in the context of return to work. The WRI comprises 17 items rated on a 4-point scale: Strongly Supports Return to Work, Supports, Interferes, Strongly Interferes. Items cover volition (personal causation about work capacity, work values, work interests), habituation (worker role, work habits), performance capacity, and work environment factors. In case study assignments involving clients with work-related injuries or vocational rehabilitation goals, WRI provides the MOHO-aligned assessment of return-to-work potential.

OSA (Occupational Self Assessment) is a client self-report measure with two columns per item: competence (how well the client perceives they perform this occupation — 4-point scale) and importance (how important this occupation is to the client — 4-point scale). The OSA directly captures the relationship between personal causation (competence ratings) and values (importance ratings) within MOHO's volition construct. In assignments, OSA results directly reflect the client's self-identified occupational priorities for the intervention plan — connecting the assessment to client-centred goal-setting.

Occupational Identity, Occupational Competence, and Occupational Adaptation

Occupational identity is a cumulative sense of who one is and wishes to become as an occupational being — it is formed through a lifetime of occupational engagement, shaped by roles experienced, relationships formed, interests pursued, and values expressed through occupation. Occupational identity represents MOHO's conceptualisation of self: not a psychological self-concept, but a specifically occupational self-understanding built through doing over time.

Occupational competence is the degree to which a person sustains a pattern of occupational participation that reflects their occupational identity. Competence is not skill — it is the ongoing enactment of the occupational self. A person who identifies as a skilled cook (occupational identity) but can no longer prepare meals independently post-stroke experiences a gap between identity and competence that OT intervention aims to bridge.

Occupational adaptation is not a fixed state or outcome — it is the dynamic, ongoing process of constructing a positive occupational identity and achieving occupational competence in the context of one's environment and life circumstances. Occupational adaptation is the answer to the question "how does a person rebuild a meaningful occupational life in the context of changed capacity, changed roles, or changed environment?" In reflective essays and case study conclusion sections, occupational adaptation provides the evaluative frame: has the intervention created conditions for the client to move toward positive occupational adaptation, or are occupational identity and competence still misaligned?

The assignment distinction: occupational identity is what a person ASPIRES to be occupationally; occupational competence is what they CAN DO that aligns with that identity; occupational adaptation is the PROCESS linking them — the ongoing reconstruction of occupational self in the face of challenge. Students who conflate occupational identity with competence, or treat adaptation as a synonym for recovery, typically lose marks on theoretical model application criteria at Level 6 and Level 7.

The MOHO Process Model — Linking Assessment to Intervention in Assignments

The MOHO Process Model guides the occupational therapist's clinical process — it is not merely a descriptive framework but a methodology for structuring OT intervention from initial assessment through to outcome evaluation. In case study assignments, the MOHO Process Model provides the logical structure for the method section: assessment → occupational formulation → goal-setting → intervention → evaluation.

Step one — developing understanding of the client — uses MOHO-based assessments (MOHOST, OPHI-II, OCAIRS, WRI, OSA) to gather construct-level data about volition, habituation, performance capacity, and environment. Step two — identifying occupational priorities and goals collaboratively — uses the assessment findings to develop an occupational formulation: a MOHO-informed narrative that explains how the client's volition, habituation, performance capacity, and environment interact to produce their current occupational engagement difficulties and strengths. Step three — implementing occupation-focused intervention — selects intervention approaches that address the constructs identified as inhibiting or restricting occupational engagement. Step four — evaluating outcomes — re-administers the baseline assessment tools (MOHOST, OPHI-II, COPM if used alongside MOHO assessments) to document change in the construct-level ratings.

The MOHO Process Model converts MOHO knowledge from a descriptive theoretical framework into a practical assignment-writing structure. The occupational formulation — MOHO's unique contribution to clinical reasoning — connects assessment findings to intervention planning with theoretical coherence, and is the element that most clearly distinguishes MOHO-informed case studies from case studies that merely name the model.

How to Apply MOHO in an OT Case Study Assignment — Step-by-Step

Applying MOHO in a case study requires a structured process that connects each MOHO construct to specific assessment findings and case data, then uses that construct-level analysis to justify the occupational formulation, goals, and intervention plan.

  1. Select appropriate MOHO assessment(s) based on the client context: MOHOST for screening occupational functioning in any setting; OPHI-II when occupational history over time is clinically relevant; OCAIRS in community mental health or forensic settings; WRI for vocational rehabilitation; OSA for client-centred goal-setting. Document the rationale for tool selection — not all MOHO assessments are appropriate for all populations.
  2. Document assessment results with correct notation: MOHOST results must state section name, rating (F/A/I/R), and clinical justification for the rating based on observation or interview evidence. OPHI-II results must state scale scores (e.g., Occupational Identity Scale 24/44) and the narrative themes from the life history interview. OCAIRS domain ratings must be stated per domain with supporting evidence.
  3. Write MOHO-informed occupational formulation linking constructs to assessment findings: "Assessment findings indicate inhibited Motivation for Occupation (MOHOST: I) reflecting diminished personal causation within volition, evidenced by the client's statement that they are 'unable to do anything for themselves anymore' despite AMPS motor logit score of 2.1 (marginally adequate for IADL). Habituation is restricted (MOHOST: R on Pattern of Occupation), with the worker role lost since injury and the domestic partner role significantly disrupted due to reduced independence in IADL."
  4. Set MOHO-aligned goals that address the constructs identified as priorities: "The client will re-establish the meal preparation occupation (habituation — domestic role), supported by graded kitchen assessment to address personal causation within volition, within four weeks."
  5. Select occupation-based interventions justified by MOHO constructs and evidence-based rationale: task-specific practice addresses performance capacity; graded success experiences address personal causation within volition; role-specific activity addresses habituation.

Writing MOHO-Informed Goals and Intervention Plans

A generic goal: "the client will improve upper limb function." A MOHO-informed goal: "the client will re-engage with the valued role of meal preparation (habituation: domestic role; volition: values and personal causation), addressing habit disruption and reduced personal causation in self-care occupations, by independently preparing a simple hot meal within 30 minutes without verbal prompting, within four weeks of weekly 45-minute kitchen assessment sessions." The second goal names the MOHO constructs being addressed (habituation, volition), specifies the occupation, states measurable performance criteria, and includes a time frame. This is the level of specificity that earns marks at Level 5 and above. For further case study writing support, see our occupational therapy case study assignment help page. Common Mistakes Students Make When Applying MOHO in Assignments

The six most common MOHO assignment errors — with corrective guidance for each:

  1. Using "motivation" instead of "volition": "Motivation" is not MOHO language. MOHO's motivational construct is "volition," comprising personal causation, values, and interests. Using "motivation" suggests unfamiliarity with MOHO's specific terminology and reduces marks on model application criteria. Always use "volition" with its three named sub-constructs.
  2. Describing MOHO rather than applying it: Writing "MOHO was used to understand the client's occupational engagement" without connecting specific constructs to specific case data is description, not application. Every construct named must be connected to assessment evidence or case data. "Volition was assessed" → no marks. "Volition — specifically personal causation — is evidenced as diminished by the client's COPM performance score of 3/10 for meal preparation, a task they previously performed independently" → distinction-level application.
  3. Stating MOHOST rating without justification: "The client scored Inhibits (I) on Motor Skills" → insufficient. "The client received an Inhibits (I) rating on Motor Skills, reflecting reduced bilateral upper limb coordination observed during tea-making assessment — dominant hand function was adequate (grip strength 15kg) but non-dominant hand stabilisation was consistently absent, requiring compensatory one-handed technique throughout" → correct documentation.
  4. Conflating occupational identity and occupational competence: Students frequently use these interchangeably. Occupational identity is what the person aspires to be occupationally (captured in OPHI-II Occupational Identity Scale scores and the life history narrative). Occupational competence is the degree to which they enact that identity through sustained occupational participation (captured in OPHI-II Occupational Competence Scale scores). A client can have a clear, intact occupational identity post-injury while having significantly reduced occupational competence — OT intervention addresses the competence gap.
  5. Reporting only objective performance capacity: Citing AMPS scores and FIM scores without addressing the subjective lived body component means the performance capacity section is only half complete. The phenomenological experience of doing — how the client describes the feeling of attempting occupation — must be documented in the occupational profile narrative using the client's own language.
  6. Treating environment as a separate add-on: Environment in MOHO is not a background section at the end of the formulation — it is an interactive shaper of occupational engagement that must be connected to volition and habituation throughout. An environment that restricts access to valued occupational forms (e.g., no community transport accessible to a wheelchair user) interacts with volition (restricts the enactment of values) and habituation (disrupts established occupational roles and routines).

MOHO vs CMOP-E — Choosing the Right Model for Your Assignment

MOHO and CMOP-E are both occupation-centred models addressing the person-environment-occupation relationship — but their emphases and assessment batteries differ in ways that matter for assignment selection and comparison essays.

Dimension MOHO CMOP-E
Origin Kielhofner (USA), 1980; 4th ed. 2008 Canadian OT theorists; CMOP-E formalised 2007 (Townsend & Polatajko)
Person components Volition, habituation, performance capacity (with lived body) Affective, cognitive, physical (no lived body concept)
Unique construct Subjective lived body in performance capacity Spirituality — personal meaning of occupation (unique philosophical contribution)
Linked assessment MOHOST, OPHI-II, OCAIRS, WRI, OSA COPM (Canadian Occupational Performance Measure) primarily
Assessment battery depth Most developed — 5 dedicated tools with detailed scoring Primarily relies on COPM; CPAF (Canadian Practice Process Framework) for process
Primary use context Mental health, physical rehabilitation, neurological, geriatric, vocational Client-centred practice broadly; Canadian and many UK OT programmes

In a model comparison essay, the key academic argument distinguishing MOHO and CMOP-E centres on: the lived body concept (MOHO unique), the spirituality construct (CMOP-E unique), and the assessment battery breadth (MOHO has greater depth). Frequently Asked Questions About MOHO Assignments

What is the difference between volition and habituation in MOHO?

Volition is the motivational dimension of MOHO — it explains why a person chooses to engage in (or avoids) occupation through three sub-constructs: personal causation (belief in one's own capacity and efficacy), values (what the person finds meaningful and feels obligated to do), and interests (what the person finds attractive and enjoyable). Habituation is the structural dimension — it explains how occupation is organised into daily life through internalised roles (social positions with associated occupational expectations) and habits (automatic, contextually cued routines). Volition is about want and meaning; habituation is about pattern and structure. Disruption to volition shows as loss of interest, loss of confidence, or reduced sense of purpose. Disruption to habituation shows as loss of routine structure, role identity confusion, or inability to organise daily occupational patterns.

How do I score the MOHOST correctly in an assignment?

The MOHOST uses a 4-point qualitative rating per section — not a numerical total score. F (Facilitates): the factor actively supports occupational engagement. A (Allows): the factor is neutral, creating no significant barriers. I (Inhibits): the factor limits or disrupts occupational engagement. R (Restricts): the factor severely compromises occupational engagement. In an assignment, each rating must be stated with the section name AND a clinical justification. Example: "The client received an Inhibits (I) rating on Pattern of Occupation, reflecting disrupted morning routines and absence of productive role occupations since job loss six months prior — the client describes having no structured daily schedule since leaving employment."

Can I use MOHO for a physical rehabilitation case study, or is it only for mental health?

MOHO is applicable across all OT practice areas — not only mental health. The common misconception that MOHO is a "mental health model" results in weaker assignments for physical rehabilitation settings. In a stroke case study, MOHO explains why a patient resists ADL practice despite adequate physical capacity (volition — diminished personal causation), why losing the worker role creates distress beyond the physical disability (habituation — role disruption), and why the subjective experience of the affected limb must be addressed alongside objective motor goals (performance capacity — lived body). MOHO's assessment tools (MOHOST, COPM used alongside MOHO constructs) are appropriate for physical rehabilitation populations across all age groups.

What is occupational adaptation in MOHO, and how do I use it in a reflective essay?

Occupational adaptation is the dynamic process by which a person constructs a positive occupational identity and achieves occupational competence over time in the context of their environment. In a reflective essay, occupational adaptation provides the evaluative frame for the entire intervention narrative: did the OT intervention create conditions for the client to move toward positive occupational adaptation — reconstructing occupational identity and building competence in their new context? A good reflective essay evaluates adaptation, not recovery. Recovery (regaining previous function) is not always possible; adaptation (building a meaningful occupational life within current capacity and context) is always the OT goal. This identity-focused frame is what Level 6 and Level 7 examiners expect in reflective work.

How does the OPHI-II differ from the MOHOST, and when should I choose each?

The OPHI-II is a semi-structured interview generating a life history narrative alongside three quantitative scales (Occupational Identity Scale 11 items, Occupational Competence Scale 9 items, Occupational Behaviour Settings Scale 9 items — each scored 1–4 per item). OPHI-II is best when the occupational history over time is clinically important — chronic conditions, major life transitions, long-term mental health. The MOHOST is an observational screening tool rated across 6 sections using F/A/I/R — best for screening current occupational functioning, particularly in inpatient or time-limited assessment contexts. In assignments: choose OPHI-II to demonstrate occupational identity change over time; choose MOHOST to document current functional occupational status across all construct domains simultaneously.
