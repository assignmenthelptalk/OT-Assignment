What Is Reflective Practice in Occupational Therapy?

Reflective practice in occupational therapy requires more than describing what happened during a placement interaction. HCPC requires registered occupational therapists to engage in reflective practice under Standards of Proficiency 14.1, which specifies that the practitioner must "draw on appropriate knowledge and skills to inform practice, including through reflective practice." This standard is not a recommendation — it is a registration requirement. RCOT's Code of Ethics reinforces this obligation through its professional development provisions, which identify ongoing reflection as an ethical duty of OT practitioners. OT students must demonstrate reflective practice capacity in academic assignments specifically because this capacity is assessed as part of the qualification pathway toward HCPC registration.

Reflective practice in OT carries a distinct academic assessment purpose: it evaluates whether the student can critically analyse their own practice, identify gaps in knowledge, and commit to professional development actions. A reflective essay differs from a general academic essay in a fundamental way — it focuses on a specific practice experience rather than a general topic, and it evaluates the student's ability to use theory to make sense of lived clinical experience. This is not a description of what the student knows about OT theory in the abstract; it is an examination of how theory illuminates a specific real interaction.

Both academic and professional obligations require reflective practice to be evidence-informed. The student who reflects on a client interaction must connect what happened to the knowledge base — OT models, HCPC standards, and published evidence — not merely to personal feeling. The assignment demands critical engagement, not therapeutic disclosure.

Why Reflective Essays Are Difficult for OT Students

OT students default to descriptive writing when first encountering reflective assignments — recounting events without analysis. Marker feedback most commonly references "insufficient analysis" and "lack of critical self-reflection" as the primary reasons for mark loss. The sections where marks are most consistently lost are the Gibbs Analysis stage (stage 4) and the Johns Influencing Factors cue (cue 3) — precisely the sections that require theoretical knowledge and HCPC standard application. These are the academically demanding sections that students skip in favour of extending their Description and Feelings content, which is easier to write but worth significantly fewer marks.

The requirement to reference HCPC standard codes in academic writing is unfamiliar to students without prior regulatory knowledge. Writing "(HCPC, 2013: Standard 9.1)" in a reflective essay feels clinical and technical against the personal voice that the reflective format encourages — yet academic markers require exactly this combination. Writing honestly about personal feelings and professional identity also challenges the academic writing conventions students have practised in literature-based essays. These tensions — personal and professional, descriptive and critical, subjective and evidence-referenced — make OT reflective assignments genuinely difficult to execute at distinction level without structured support.

Reflective Models for OT Assignments — Gibbs, Schon, and Johns

Occupational therapy reflective assignments require the application of a structured reflective model to organise the student's engagement with practice experience. The three models this service applies — Gibbs Reflective Cycle, Schon's Reflection-in-Action and Reflection-on-Action, and Johns Model of Structured Reflection — each structures the reflective essay differently, produces different analytical emphases, and suits different OT assignment contexts. The Kolb Experiential Learning Cycle (Concrete Experience, Reflective Observation, Abstract Conceptualisation, Active Experimentation) provides a supplementary framework occasionally required in UK OT programmes, though less commonly than the three primary models.

Model selection requires attention to assignment level and scenario type. Gibbs suits students new to reflective writing or writing about straightforward practice scenarios — its six-stage structure provides explicit scaffolding. Schon suits documenting real-time clinical decision-making during complex interventions, and is most appropriate at Level 5–7 when students have sufficient clinical experience to identify genuine in-action adjustments. Johns suits ethically complex scenarios involving professional dilemmas or conflicting values — its five cue structure is designed to surface the underlying knowledge and assumptions that shaped the decision. Where placement journals do not specify a model, Gibbs is the safest default for BSc students; Johns is preferred for MSc and OTD assignments.

Gibbs Reflective Cycle — Six Stages for OT Placement Reflection

Gibbs Reflective Cycle structures the reflective essay across six stages, each corresponding to a paragraph section with a stage-identifying topic sentence. Stage 1 (Description) provides a factual account of the specific practice scenario: who was present, what happened, what was the student's role. Stage 2 (Feelings) provides an honest account of the emotional response during and after the event. Stage 3 (Evaluation) addresses what went well and what did not go well in the interaction or intervention. Stage 4 (Analysis) is the academic core of the essay: which theoretical framework or OT model explains what occurred, which HCPC standard is relevant to this scenario, and what research evidence supports a different approach — citations must appear in this stage. Stage 5 (Conclusion) addresses what could have been done differently. Stage 6 (Action Plan) commits to a specific professional development action for future similar scenarios.

The word count allocation across Gibbs stages is a critical marking principle. Description and Feelings combined should occupy no more than 15% of the total word count. Evaluation warrants approximately 15%. Analysis should receive 45–50% of the total word count — this is where HCPC standard codes, OT theory citations, and evidence-based arguments belong. Conclusion and Action Plan together warrant 20–25%. Students who allocate word count proportionally to effort rather than to academic weight consistently over-invest in Description (the easiest section to write) and under-invest in Analysis (the section worth the most marks). A 2,000-word reflective essay at distinction level allocates 900–1,000 words to the Analysis stage.

Schon's Reflection-in-Action and Reflection-on-Action for OT Practice

Schon's model addresses two distinct temporal modes of reflection that are directly applicable to clinical OT practice. Reflection-in-Action describes the cognitive process occurring during the clinical encounter itself: recognising an unexpected outcome, adjusting approach in real time, testing a new hypothesis within the session. In OT academic writing, Reflection-in-Action is documented retrospectively as first-person narrative: "At the moment the client refused the dressing task, I recognised that my initial assumption about motivation was incorrect — I adjusted my approach by offering the client a choice of starting point, shifting from a structured sequence to client-led ordering."

Reflection-on-Action is the retrospective analysis conducted after the session: identifying which knowledge framed the initial approach, why the in-action adjustment was made, and what theoretical framework explains the decision. In academic writing, this takes the form of systematic self-inquiry: "Reflecting on the interaction, I identify that my clinical reasoning drew on procedural reasoning regarding the physical task demands of dressing without adequately applying narrative reasoning to the client's occupational history — specifically, the client's strong occupational identity around self-determination, which MOHO's volition construct would have predicted as a motivational barrier to prescriptive task sequencing."

Schon is most applicable at Level 5–7 when students have sufficient clinical experience to document genuine in-action adjustments and name the theoretical knowledge that should have informed their initial approach. An in-action adjustment without a named knowledge framework — "I adjusted my approach because it didn't feel right" — is not Schon reflection; it is experiential response without academic depth.

Johns Model of Structured Reflection — Five Cues for Ethically Complex OT Scenarios

Johns Model of Structured Reflection, authored by Christopher Johns in 1994 and revised through to the fifth edition of Becoming a Reflective Practitioner (2017), structures reflection around five cue categories. Cue 1 (Description) describes the experience, including the significant factors that contributed to it. Cue 2 (Reflection) addresses: what was the student trying to achieve, why did they respond as they did, and what were the consequences for the client, for themselves, and for others. Cue 3 (Influencing Factors) asks: what internal factors influenced the student's decision — prior knowledge, values, assumptions, emotional state — and what knowledge was available that should have informed them. Cue 4 (Could I Have Dealt Better) examines what other choices existed and what their consequences would have been. Cue 5 (Learning) addresses how this experience changes the student's ways of knowing and what future practice will look like differently.

Cue 3, Influencing Factors, is the most academically demanding and the most frequently underdeveloped in student submissions. This is the cue where HCPC standard references belong — the student must identify the specific regulatory or theoretical knowledge that should have informed the decision but did not, or that was applied in ways that could be made more explicit. In OT, this means citing OT models by name (MOHO volition construct, CMOP-E spirituality), HCPC Standards of Proficiency by code, and peer-reviewed evidence. Students who skip or thin this cue produce a personally insightful journal entry that fails academically — the examiner cannot award marks for reflective learning that lacks theoretical grounding.

Johns is particularly appropriate for scenarios involving professional dilemmas (conflicting client autonomy with duty of care), ethical decisions (mandatory reporting, safeguarding triggers), or complex MDT interactions where professional role boundaries required navigation.

HCPC Standards of Proficiency in OT Reflective Writing

HCPC Standards of Proficiency set the professional competency baseline that OT reflective essays must reference — not as a compliance exercise but as evidence that the student understands the regulatory framework governing their future practice. The Standards of Proficiency for Occupational Therapists (HCPC, 2013) identify fourteen domains. For reflective writing assignments, the most relevant standards are those that directly address professional decision-making, scope of practice, and learning from experience. The standard citation format in academic writing is "(HCPC, 2013: Standard [number.sub-number])" or as directed by institutional Harvard referencing requirements.

Standard 1.1 requires the practitioner to "understand the need to act in the best interests of service users at all times" — this standard applies when reflecting on a clinical decision that required balancing client autonomy with safety, or when a client declined recommended intervention. Standard 2.1 requires the practitioner to "be able to practise safely and effectively within their scope of practice" — this applies when reflecting on recognising and responding to the limits of personal competence. Standard 4.1 requires the practitioner to "be able to assess a situation and plan and implement interventions" — this applies when reflecting on clinical reasoning during assessment and planning activities. Standard 9.1 requires the practitioner to "be able to work appropriately with others" — this applies to reflections on MDT interactions, collaborative working, or carer involvement. Standard 10.1 requires the practitioner to "be able to keep accurate, legible records and use these records appropriately" — this applies when reflecting on documentation practices. Standard 14.1, the foundational standard for all reflective writing, requires the practitioner to "draw on appropriate knowledge and skills to inform practice, including through reflective practice." Standard 14.2 requires the practitioner to "recognise the value of learning, reflection and professional development" — this is the standard cited in the Action Plan stage of Gibbs or the Learning cue of Johns.

Levels of Reflection — Descriptive, Analytical, and Critical Reflective Writing

Most OT students default to descriptive reflection when first encountering reflective assignments — the Level 1 response that recounts what happened without analysis. This is the most common student output at Level 4, and it is the output most commonly referenced in negative marker feedback. The three levels of reflection quality form a quality spectrum that directly determines marks.

Level 1 (Descriptive) recounts events without analysis, theory, or learning identified. An example of Level 1 writing: "I carried out a COPM interview with the client and identified three occupational performance issues. The client was pleased with the interaction." This passage records a fact and a reaction — it does not analyse what the therapeutic interaction produced, why the client responded as they did, or what OT knowledge framed the approach. No marks are awarded for analytical criteria in Level 1 writing.

Level 2 (Analytical) connects the experience to theory or evidence and identifies what worked and why. An example of Level 2 writing: "The collaborative approach to priority setting through the COPM interview reflects the client-centred principle articulated in CMOP-E's focus on occupational engagement; the client's positive response may reflect the heightened personal causation (MOHO) that results from self-directed goal identification." This passage names an OT model, connects it to evidence, and interprets the observation — this is analytical engagement.

Level 3 (Critical/Transformative) demonstrates critical self-analysis by challenging underlying assumptions, questioning professional norms, and identifying structural or power-related factors. An example of Level 3 writing: "On reflection, I recognise that my initial assumption that the client would prioritise self-care over leisure reflects a medically-influenced view of rehabilitation outcomes. The client's stated priority — resuming social participation — challenges this assumption and aligns with RCOT's person-centred practice standard and the concept of occupational justice, which recognises the right of individuals to engage in occupations meaningful to them." This passage challenges the student's own assumptions, names the professional standard, and introduces a critical theoretical concept. Level 3 reflection demonstrates critical self-analysis and is required for distinction marks (70%+) at Level 6 and Level 7.

Structure of an OT Practice Placement Journal — Per-Entry Format and Portfolio Arc

A practice placement journal differs from a standalone reflective essay in one critical way: it documents professional development across a placement block rather than analysing a single scenario in depth. The per-entry format and the portfolio arc across entries are both assessed components — not only the quality of individual entries.

Each journal entry documents a specific practice scenario using the following structure: a title identifying the placement date, setting, and scenario type; a scenario description of no more than 100–150 words — this is not a full Gibbs Description stage but a concise contextual statement; a reflective analysis of 300–600 words constituting the analytical and critical content; at least one HCPC Standard of Proficiency referenced with code and descriptor; at least one OT model or evidence citation; and a specific, measurable, forward-facing professional development action. Entries that substitute generic reflection ("I learned about communication skills today") for specific clinical analysis fail on multiple assessment criteria simultaneously.

The portfolio arc requires that entries show development over time. Entry 1 should establish a baseline of professional self-awareness. Entries 5–6 should demonstrate development from earlier entries with explicit cross-referencing: "In my entry dated [date], I identified uncertainty about MDT communication — having now participated in three MDT meetings, I am now able to..." The final entry should synthesise learning across the full placement block. Common portfolio lengths run 5–10 entries totalling 4,000–8,000 words. Some programmes require evidence that journal entries were discussed in clinical supervision, with a supervisor initials or dated confirmation on entries.

Common Placement Scenarios for OT Reflective Journal Entries

The scenarios most commonly reflected on in OT placement journals each suit different reflective models and activate different HCPC standards. First professional contact with a client — appropriate for Gibbs — generates emotional response content relevant to establishing a therapeutic relationship and activates Standards 1.1 and 4.1. Participating in an MDT meeting — appropriate for Johns — requires examination of professional role awareness and activates Standard 9.1. Recognising the limits of competence during an assessment — appropriate for Schon's Reflection-in-Action — requires documentation of the in-action recognition and response, activating Standard 2.1.

An ethical dilemma, such as a client declining recommended equipment or a family member requesting information about the client without consent, suits Johns' structure and activates Standard 1.1 alongside RCOT Code of Ethics principles. Discharge planning interactions, which require conditional reasoning about the client's future occupational life, activate Standard 4.1. A positive therapeutic outcome that consolidates professional identity — what placement educators sometimes call a "landmark moment" — is often used in the final portfolio entry to synthesise professional development across the placement block. Students should select scenarios that show variety — not five iterations of the same type of assessment interaction, but a range of interaction types, reasoning challenges, and emotional responses that demonstrate the breadth of professional learning.

Acute Hospital OT Placement Reflective Journal Help

Acute hospital OT placements generate a distinctive reflective writing challenge: the pace of clinical work — rapid ADL assessment on a ward with high patient turnover, FIM scoring, MDT ward rounds, and discharge planning — produces multiple scenario options for journal entries but limits the depth of individual therapeutic relationships available for sustained reflection. Students on acute placements frequently struggle to find a single scenario with sufficient narrative depth for a full Gibbs or Johns entry.

In acute OT reflective journals, the most productive scenarios include the first ADL assessment under time pressure, participation in a MDT ward round where the OT's assessment findings required communication to medical colleagues, and discharge planning decisions where assessment data was incomplete. HCPC Standard 9.1 (MDT working), Standard 10.1 (record-keeping in acute documentation systems), and Standard 2.1 (scope of practice — recognising when a patient's presentation requires referral rather than direct OT intervention) are the standards most commonly activated in acute placement scenarios. Community OT, Mental Health, and Paediatric Placement Reflective Help

Community OT placements involve home visit scenarios, carer communication, equipment prescription, and community independence assessments — all of which generate reflective material around client autonomy versus safety (Standard 1.1) and the institutional environment factors that shape what is possible for a client. Mental health OT placements activate MOHO's volition construct most directly — personal causation in clients experiencing psychosis or depression is a recurring clinical concern that produces high-quality Level 3 reflective writing when connected to MOHO theory and HCPC Standards 1.1 and 3.1. Recovery Model principles, professional boundary maintenance, and the therapeutic relationship in mental health settings each generate appropriate scenarios for Johns' ethically complex cue structure.

Paediatric OT placements require reflective journal entries that address developmental observation, parent and carer communication, and school environment contexts. Family-centred practice introduces consent and information-sharing complexity that activates Standard 9.1 and Standard 1.1. Standard writing challenges in paediatric placement journals include maintaining appropriate scope around child protection awareness and distinguishing direct clinical observation from parental report. Linking OT Theory to Reflective Practice — Models, Evidence, and Professional Identity

Theory integration in OT reflective essays requires connecting a named OT model to the specific scenario — not mentioning a model by name and moving on, but showing how the model's constructs explain what was observed. MOHO's personal causation construct explains a client who refused a task from a motivational rather than a physical explanation. CMOP-E's environment domain explains a home visit in which access barriers were the primary occupational performance constraint. Citing a BJOT or AJOT paper in the Analysis stage of Gibbs establishes that the student connects the scenario to the published evidence base — not merely to theoretical frameworks.

Professional identity development in reflective writing requires the student to engage with RCOT Professional Standards 2021 as an objective anchor for self-evaluation. Writing "I felt more confident this week" is personal development writing; writing "This interaction required me to enact the client-centred principle specified in RCOT Professional Standards 2021, and I recognise that my earlier tendency to direct the interaction toward clinical goals I had pre-identified conflicts with this standard" is professional identity reflection that examiners can assess. Frequently Asked Questions — OT Reflective Essay and Practice Placement Journal

Which reflective model should I use for my OT placement journal — Gibbs, Schon, or Johns?

Check your assignment brief first — most programmes specify the required model. If unspecified: use Gibbs if you are at Level 4 or 5 and new to reflective writing, as its six-stage structure provides clear scaffolding for any placement scenario. Use Schon if you want to capture real-time clinical decision-making during a complex intervention — this model suits Level 5–6 students with meaningful placement experience who can name the knowledge that informed an in-action adjustment. Use Johns for ethically complex scenarios involving professional dilemmas, client autonomy challenges, or conflicting values — Johns' cue 3 (Influencing Factors) is particularly effective at surfacing underlying assumptions. Multiple models can be used across a portfolio — one model per entry is not required if the programme brief does not specify otherwise.

How many HCPC standards do I need to reference in a reflective essay?

Most OT programme marking rubrics require a minimum of one HCPC Standard of Proficiency per reflective entry. For distinction-level work, two or more standards should be referenced with explicit connection to the scenario — not simply listed. The most commonly applicable standards for placement reflective journals are 1.1 (best interests), 2.1 (scope of practice), 9.1 (working with others), 10.1 (record-keeping), and 14.1 (reflective practice). Each standard must be cited accurately — "(HCPC, 2013: Standard 1.1)" — and the specific relevance to the scenario must be explained. Vague references such as "I followed HCPC standards" receive no marks on analytical criteria.

What is the difference between a reflective essay and a practice placement journal in OT?

A reflective essay is typically a standalone academic assignment that reflects on one significant practice experience in depth, usually 1,500–3,000 words, submitted as a single document. A practice placement journal is a portfolio of multiple shorter reflective entries — typically 5–10 entries of 500–1,000 words each — written throughout a placement block, demonstrating professional development over time. The essay format allows deeper analysis of a single scenario; the journal format requires showing growth and change across entries. Both require the same components — reflective model, HCPC standards, theory linkage — but at different depths per entry.

Can I reflect on a challenging or difficult placement experience in my OT reflective essay?

Yes — challenging experiences often produce the strongest reflective essays because they generate genuine analysis. Ethical dilemmas, difficult client interactions, moments of uncertainty, or situations where you recognised the limits of your competence are all appropriate for academic reflection. The key academic requirement is that the analysis — Gibbs stage 4 or Johns cue 3 — connects the challenge to specific HCPC standards and OT theory, not only to personal feelings. A client refusing treatment, a safeguarding concern, or a disagreement within an MDT are all legitimate and high-value scenarios. This service can structure any placement scenario using the appropriate reflective model framework.

Does my OT reflective essay need to include citations and a reference list?

Yes — citations are required in the Analysis stage (Gibbs stage 4) and the Influencing Factors or Theory sections of other models. Citations in OT reflective essays should reference: HCPC Standards of Proficiency (cited as HCPC, 2013); RCOT professional standards or code of ethics where relevant; OT theoretical model sources (Kielhofner, 2008 for MOHO; Townsend and Polatajko, 2013 for CMOP-E); and peer-reviewed evidence that supports or challenges the approach taken in practice, drawing on AJOT, BJOT, and OTseeker sources. A reference list in Harvard format is required. The reflective style of writing does not exempt the assignment from academic citation requirements.
